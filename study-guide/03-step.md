### Polyamides

A polyamide is a polymer with repeating units linked by amide bonds which is naturally the reaction product from carboxylic acid and amine. Protein is a type of polyamide. Nylon is a family of synthetic polyamide, usually linking aliphatic or semi-aromatic groups.

::: left
![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/53/Polyamide_Amidegroup_V.1.png =300x)
:::

**Production**

Nylon 66 and related polyamides are condensation polymers forms from equal parts of diamine and dicarboxylic acids. The numbers in the name indicates the monomer units involved. For example, 66 means the diamine is hexamethylene diamine which has 6 carbon atoms and the dicarboxylic acid is adipic acid which also has 6 carbon atoms. In case of Nylon 610, the diamine has 6 carbon atoms while the dicarboxylic acid has 10 carbon atoms. Similarly, there are products named Nylon 46, 410, 510, 612, 1010, etc. There are also Nylon products with only one number, such as Nylon 6 which is produced using an amino acid that has 6 carbon atom molecule with a carboxylic acid group at one end and an amino group the other end. Nylon 6 can also be produced by ring-opening polymerization of lactam. 

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8e/Synth%C3%A8se_Nylon_6%2C6.png =600x)

In case of Nylon 66, the stoichiometric balance between reactive groups is readily obtained by precipitating the 1:1 ammonium salt from ethanol. This compound is sometimes called a nylon salt. The salt/polymer equilibrium is more favorable to the production of polymer than in the case of polyesters, so this reaction is often carried out in a sealed tube or autoclave at about 200 °C until a fairly high extent of reaction is reached; then the temperature is raised and the water driven off to attain high molecular weight polymer. 

**Properties**

The planar amide ($\ce{-CO-NH -}$) groups are very polar, so nylon forms multiple hydrogen bonds among adjacent strands. 

Below $T_m$, amorphous regions alternate with regions which are lamellar crystals. The amorphous regions contribute elasticity, and the crystalline regions contribute strength and rigidity. Because the nylon backbone is so regular and symmetrical, especially if all the amide bonds are in the trans configuration, nylons often have high crystallinity and make excellent fibers.

Nylons are hygroscopic and will absorb or desorb moisture as a function of the ambient humidity.

All nylons are susceptible to hydrolysis, especially by strong acids, a reaction essentially the reverse of their synthesis. The molecular weight of nylon products so attacked drops, and cracks form quickly at the affected zones.

**Applications**

Nylon 66 is frequently used when high mechanical strength, rigidity, good stability under heat and/or chemical resistance are required. Fiber is the most important form of application but other types such as film and bulk plastics are also available.

### Polyesters

Polyester is a category of polymers that contain one or two ester linkages in every repeat unit of their main chain. The most commonly used polyester is polyethylene terephthalate (PET). Polyesters include naturally occurring chemicals, such as in plants and insects. Natural polyesters and a few synthetic ones are biodegradable, but most synthetic polyesters are not. Synthetic polyesters are used extensively in clothing.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ee/Polyethyleneterephthalate.svg =300x)

**Production**

Since the methyl ester of a dicarboxylic acid is often more soluble and easier to purify than the diacid itself, the dimethyl terephthalate process was employed first and lasted for a long time. Now, purified terephthalic acid is more widely available, thus the teraphthalic acid process is also used. 

In the dimethyl terephthalate (DMT) process, DMT and excess ethylene glycol (MEG) are transesterified in the melt at 150–200 °C with a basic catalyst. Methanol ($\ce{CH3OH}$) is removed by distillation to drive the reaction forward. Excess MEG is distilled off at higher temperature with the aid of vacuum. The second transesterification step proceeds at 270–280 °C, with continuous distillation of MEG as well.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b5/PET_by_Transesterification_V1.svg =400x)

In the terephthalic acid process, MEG and PTA are esterified directly at moderate pressure (2.7–5.5 bar) and high temperature (220–260 °C). Water is eliminated in the reaction, and it is also continuously removed by distillation. 

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/83/PET_by_Polycondensation_V1.svg =400x)

**Properties**

PET is widely used as drink bottles and fibers for clothes. It has fairly good mechanical properties and durability, but less strong compared to Nylon. 

PET is subject to degradation during processing. If the moisture level is too high, hydrolysis will reduce the molecular weight by chain scission, resulting in brittleness.

### Polyimides

## Kinetics{{attrs[#blk-qey49l78h609]}}

Assumption of equal reactivity
: The reactivity of a functional group is independent of the size of the molecule containing it. 

- Early work by Carothers, Flory, and others explored the influence of molecular size on reactivity by studying esterification and saponification rates.
- These reactions exhibit an initial dependence on molecular size for small molecules.
- The rate constants rapidly converge to a constant value, independent of molecular size, as the molecules get larger.
- In carboxylic acid esterification, for example, the rate constants vary for acetic, propionic, and butyric acids.
- However, the rate constants become consistent for carboxylic acids with 4 to 18 carbon atoms.
- This principle, initially observed in non-polymeric systems, has been extended to polymerization reactions.

### Polyesterification

The kinetics and rates of step-growth polymerization can be described using a polyesterification mechanism. The simple esterification is an acid-catalyzed process in which protonation of the acid is followed by interaction with the alcohol to produce an ester and water. A few assumptions needed with this kinetic model: 
1. Water (or any other condensation product) is efficiently removed; 
2. The functional group reactivities are independent of chain length; 
3. Each step only involves one alcohol and one acid.



#### Self-catalyzed polyesterification

If no acid catalyst is added, the reaction will still proceed because the acid can act as its own catalyst. The rate of condensation at any time $t$ can then be derived from the rate of disappearance of $\ce{COOH}$ groups and

$$rate=\frac{-d[\ce{COOH}]}{dt}=k[\ce{COOH}]^2[\ce{OH}]$$

The second-order $[\ce{COOH}]$ term arises from its use as a catalyst, and $k$ is the rate constant. For a system with equivalent quantities of acid and alcohol, 

$$c=[\ce{COOH}]=[\ce{OH}]$$

the functional group concentration can be written simply as

$$rate=\frac{-dc}{dt}=kc^3$$

After integration and substitution from Carothers equation, the final form is the following

$$\frac{1}{(1-p)^2}=2ktc^2+1=DP^2_n$$

For a self-catalyzed system, the number average degree of polymerization $DP_n$ grows proportionally with $\sqrt{t}$.

#### External catalyzed polyesterification

The uncatalyzed reaction is rather slow, and a high $DP_n$ is not readily attained. In the presence of an externally added catalyst, there is an acceleration of the rate, and the kinetic expression is altered to

$$\frac{-d[\ce{COOH}]}{dt}=k[\ce{COOH}][\ce{OH}]$$

which is kinetically first order in each functional group. Hence,

$$\frac{-dc}{dt}=kc^2$$

for equal amount of acid and alcohol, and integration gives finally

$$\frac{1}{1-p}=1+ckt=DP_n$$

For an externally catalyzed system, the number average degree of polymerization grows proportionally with $t$.

## Molecular weight of the product{{attrs[#blk-v0wlf7b37is8]}}

To understand better, use the following simulator:

> [OVESET -- Step growth polymerization](https://colab.research.google.com/github/wangyu16/PolymerScienceEducation/blob/master/OVESET/02_Step_Growth_Polymerization.ipynb)

In step-growth polymerization, the keys to find the number average degree of polymerization ($DP_n$) are:
1. $DP_n$ equals the ratio between the initial and final numbers of molecules;
2. The final number of molecules equas the initial number subtract the number of reactions have happened. 

Be careful, the number of molecules and the number of functional groups are two different concepts. 

### Stoichiometric balance

#### Number average degree of polymerization

Imagine a simple step-growth polymerization of AB type monomers. The initial number of monomers is $N_0$. The initial number of each type of functional group should be the same, 

$$N_0=N_{A,0}=N_{B,0}$$

A reaction between A and B 
- reduces the number of both types of functional groups by one;
- If intermolecular reaction is omitted, the number of molecules should also decrease by one. 

Thus, at time $t$, the number of molecules becomes

$$N_t=N_{A,t}=N_{B,t}$$

The extent of reaction $p$ is defined as the conversion of the functional groups. In this case, it does not matter A or B type of functional group, since they always have the same number. 

$$p=\frac{N_{A,0}-N_{A,t}}{N_{A,0}}$$

The number average degree of polymerization ($DP_n$) equals to the initial numbers of monomers devided by the number of molecules at time $t$. 

$$DP_n = \frac{N_0}{N_t} = \frac{N_{A,0}}{N_{A,t}} = \frac{1}{1-p}$$

In order to get high $DP_n$, the extent of reaction must be close to 1. If $p=0.99$, the $DP_n$ is 100, which is usually still too low for commercial use. 

If we consider a polymerization of AA and BB type monomers with exactly equal mole fraction, the above conclusion does not change. 

#### Molecular weight distribution

**The number fraction of $i$-mers**

Consider the polymerization of AB type monomers. Assume the extent of reaction is $p$, the number fraction of $i$-mers is $x(i)$. 

The extent of reaction is also the conversion of functional group A. Thus, the probability that a randomly chosen A functional group has reacted equals to $p$. In an $i$-mer, there must be $i-1$ A functional groups that have reacted and one not reacted. Thus, the probability is

$$x(i) = p^{i-1}(1-p)$$

It is easy to prove that the sum of $x(i)$ equals 1. 

$$
\begin{align*}
\sum_{i=1}^\infty x(i) & = \sum_{i=1}^\infty p^{i-1}(1-p) \\
& = (1-p)\sum_{i=0}^\infty p^i \\
& = (1-p)\times\frac{1}{1-p} \\
& = 1
\end{align*}
$$

The **number average degree of polymerization** can be calculated as the first moment of the distribution: 

$$
\begin{align*}
DP_n & = \sum_{i=1}^\infty i x(i) \\
& = \sum_{i=1}^\infty i p^{i-1}(1-p) \\
& = (1-p)\sum_{i=1}^\infty ip^{i-1} \\
& = (1-p)\times\frac{1}{(1-p)^2} \\
& = \frac{1}{1-p}
\end{align*}
$$

The **weight average degree of polymerization** can be calculated as the second moment devided by the first moment: 

$$
\begin{align*}
DP_w & = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^\infty i^2 x(i)}{\sum_{i=1}^\infty i x(i)} \\
& = (1-p)\sum_{i=1}^\infty i^2x(i) \\
& = (1-p)^2\sum_{i=1}^\infty i^2p^{i-1} \\
& = (1-p)^2\times\frac{1+p}{(1-p)^3} \\
& = \frac{1+p}{1-p}
\end{align*}
$$

Another way to derive the expression is as follows: 

1. The weight fraction of $i$-mer is 

$$w(i) = \frac{i x(i)}{\sum_{i=1}^\infty i x(i)}$$

2. The weight average molecular weight is the mean value 

$$DP_w = \sum_{i=1}^\infty iw(i)$$

The **molecular weight distribution** is represented by $M_w/M_n$:

$$M_w/M_n = \frac{DP_w}{DP_n} = 1+p$$

When $p$ is close to 1, the $M_w/M_n$ value is close to 2. 

The above discussion is valid for polymerization of AB type monomers and polymerization of AA + BB type monomers with exact stoichiometric balance. 

### Stoichiometric imbalance  

Regarding the polymerization of AA + BB type monomers, consider to scenario: 

1. the two types of monomers do not have a stoichiometric balance; 
2. small amount of monofunctional monomers exist in the system. 

How would these factors influence the molecular weight of the resulting polymers? 

#### Simple stoichiometric imbalance

Assume AA monomer is less than BB monomer. The ratio between the number of A and B functional groups is:

$$r = \frac{N_{A,0}}{N_{B,0}}$$

We have the correlation

$$N_0 = \frac{N_{A,0}}{2} + \frac{N_{B,0}}{2} = \frac{(1+1/r)N_{A,0}}{2}$$

where $N_0$ is the total number of monomers at time 0. 

The extent of reaction ($p$) is defined as the conversion of A functional groups. 

The number average degree of polymerization can be calculated as

$$DP_n = \frac{N_0}{N_t}$$

Neglecting intramolecular reactions, each reaction would reduce the number of molecules by one. The number of reactions happend is 

$$pN_{A,0}$$

Thus,

$$N_t = N_0 - pN_{A,0} = \frac{(1+1/r)N_{A,0}}{2} - pN_{A,0}$$


The number average degree of polymerization is

$$DP_n = \frac{N_0}{N_t} = \frac{\frac{(1+1/r)N_{A,0}}{2}}{\frac{(1+1/r)N_{A,0}}{2} - pN_{A,0}} =\frac{1+1/r}{1+1/r-2p} = \frac{r+1}{r+1-2rp}
$$

When $r=1$, we get $DP_n = 1/(1-p)$, which agrees the above conclusions.

When $r=0.99$ and $p=1$, we have $DP_n = 199$, which means even if all A type of functional groups are consumed, the $DP_n$ is limited to 199. 

#### Presence of monofunctional monomer

If initially there are AA, BB and RB type of monomers, where RB are monofunctional monomers, what would be $DP_n$ at reaction extent $p$? Assume the total number of A functional groups is less than that of B. 

The total number of monomers is 

$$N_0 = N_{RB,0} + \frac{N_{A,0}}{2} + \frac{N_{B,0}}{2}$$

where $N_{B,0}$ means the number of B type functional groups originated from BB type monomers only. 

At time $t$ when reaction extent is $p$, the number of molecules becomes

$$N_t = N_0 - pN_{A,0}$$

Thus $DP_n$ is 

$$DP_n = \frac{N_0}{N_t} = \frac{2N_{RB,0}+N_{A,0}+N_{B,0}}{2N_{RB,0}+N_{A,0}+N_{B,0}-2pN_{A,0}}$$

Define a ratio $r'$ as

$$r' = \frac{N_{A,0}}{2N_{RB,0} + N_{B,0}}$$

we get

$$DP_n = \frac{1+r'}{1+r'-2r'p}$$

The meaning of $r'$ is not straightforward. It is kind of the ratio between A and B, but the number of RB is counted twice. Regardless, the formula for $DP_n$ is quite simple, which has the same format as the above case.

### Gelation theory of Carothers

Define the number-average functionality $f_{av}$ as the total number of functional groups devided by the total number of molecules. If there are $N_0$ molecules, there would be $N_0f_{av}$ functional groups.  

Since each reaction would reduce the number of molecules by one (intramolecular raction is omitted), at time $t$ if the number of moleclues is $N$, the number of functional groups that have reacted is $2(N_0 - N)$.

Define the extend of reaction $p$ as the probability that a functional group has reacted, i.e.,

$$p = \frac{2(N_0 - N)}{N_0f_{av}}$$

The average degree of polymerization is

$$DP_n = \frac{N_0}{N} = \frac{2}{2-pf_{av}}$$

Thus,

$$\boxed{p_c \geq \frac{2}{f_{av}}}$$

leads to

$$DP_n \to \infty$$

Note, gelation would occur before the predicted $p_c$.

Carothers gelation theory can be applied to both polymerization of pre-existing linear polymers each with $N$ functionalitites and polymerization of multifunctional small molecules.

Assume there are many pre-existing linear chains each has $N$ monomer units.

Reactions can take place between any two monomer units on these polymer chains to create a cross-link.

Define the extent of reaction $p$, i.e., the percent of monomer units have reacted.

On average, there are $Np$ cross-link points on each polymer chain.

The question is, on average, how many chains are linked together at this moment. Or, start from one chain, how likely to find another chain linked to this chain, and then another chain, and so on. If the number approaches infinity, the system is crosslinked, the extent of reaction is called the critical extent of reaction $p_c$.

$$p_c = \frac{1}{N-1} \approx \frac{1}{N}$$

If the pre-existing chains have a molecular distribution. Use weight average degree of polymerization $N_w$ to replace $N$ and the way to calculate $p_c$ remains the same.

$$p_c \approx \frac{1}{N_w}$$

### Stockmayer and Flory theory of gelation.

Think about a reaction of AA, BB, AB, Af and Bf. Assume B groups are present in excess.
1. The ratio of the intial number of A to B is $r = N_A^0/N_B^0$.
2. The fraction of A present in multifunctional molecules is defined as
$$\rho=\frac{fN_{Af}^0}{N_A^0}$$
where $N^0_{Af}$ means the number of Af type molecules and $N_A^0$ means the number of all A type functional groups. 
3. The extent of reaction $p$ is based on A.
4. The branching coefficient $\alpha$ is the probability that a chain segment is capped at both ends by a branch unit.

#### Calculation of the branching coefficient

Calculate the probability that starts from an Af, reacts with BB, AA, BB, AA, ..., BB, and finally another Af.

* The first step has a probability $p$.
* The second step $rp(1-\rho)$.
* Next step, again $p$.
* Then again $rp(1-\rho)$.
* The final step $rp\rho$.

The overall probability is

$$p[rp(1-\rho)p]^irp\rho$$

The branching coefficient gives the probability of a chain segment being capped by potential branch points

$$
\begin{align*}
\alpha & = \sum_{i=0}^\infty rp^2\rho[rp^2(1-\rho)]^i \\
& = \frac{rp^2\rho}{1-rp^2(1-\rho)}
\end{align*}
$$

#### Gel point

Consider these linear strands each with two Af end caps. Start from one strand how likely to find another strand linked to it, and then another one, and go on.

Start from one Af chain end, there are $f-1$ A groups need to be considered, each one has the probability $\alpha$ that it is reacted. If the value $(f-1)\alpha$ is less than one, the chance to go from one strand to another and another vanishes sooner or later. If the value $(f-1)\alpha$ is greater than one, it is likely to find infinity number of strands that has been linked together. Thus, the critical extent of reaction is given by

$$\alpha_c = \frac{1}{f-1}$$

thus

$$\boxed{p_c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{r + r\rho(f-2)}}}$$

The formation of loops and the reduced reactivity due to steric hinderance are omitted. The actually gelation point may occur later than the prediction.

#### Molecular weight averages

In the system generated from AA, BB and Af, we restrict to the condition of $N_A^0 =  N_B^0$. The average functionality of a monomer $\langle f\rangle$ is defined as

$$\langle f\rangle \equiv \frac{\sum_i n_if_i}{\sum_i n_i}$$

The number average degree of polymerization of the sol fraction is given by the Carothers equation.

An analysis of the polymerization of Af monomers gives the number average and weight average degree of polymerization

$$N_n = \frac{2}{2-\alpha f}$$

and

$$N_w = \frac{1+\alpha}{1-\alpha(f-1)}$$

At the gel point $\alpha_c = 1/(f-1)$, $N_w$ becomes infinite, whereas $N_n$ remains finite.

As $\alpha\to\alpha_c$, the ratio $N_w/N_n$ indicates a divergence of the dispersity. It emphasizes that in addition to the network molecule, or gel fraction, of essentially infinite molecular weight, there are still many other molecules present at the gel point, the sol fraction.

#### Sol-gel fractions

In polymer science, the terms "sol" and "gel" fractions refer to two distinct components of a polymer network, typically encountered when studying crosslinked polymers or during processes like gelation.

The **sol fraction** is the portion of a polymer sample that remains soluble in a solvent. It consists of low molecular weight chains, uncrosslinked molecules, or loosely connected polymer fragments that don’t form part of the larger, interconnected network. Essentially, these are the bits that can still dissolve and be extracted because they aren’t tied into the permanent structure. Think of it as the "loose" or "free-floating" part of the system.

The **gel fraction**, on the other hand, is the insoluble portion—the crosslinked, three-dimensional network of polymer chains that forms a solid-like structure. Once the polymer reaches a certain level of crosslinking (past the gel point), it transitions into this gel state, where it can no longer dissolve but might swell in a solvent. This is the "fixed" or "solid" part that gives the material its structural integrity.

These concepts are key in understanding how polymers behave, especially in applications like hydrogels, rubbers, or coatings. For example, in a crosslinked rubber, the gel fraction determines its strength and elasticity, while the sol fraction might indicate incomplete crosslinking or leftover monomers. Researchers often measure these fractions experimentally by soaking a sample in a solvent and weighing what dissolves (sol) versus what remains (gel).
