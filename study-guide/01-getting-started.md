## Definitions of polymer and macromolecule{{attrs[#blk-is1u7r7i6fhc]}}

Polymer
: The term "polymer" derives from Greek πολύς (polus) 'many, much' and μέρος (meros) 'part'.

Typically, a polymer consists of small *repeating units* that are hold together by chemical bond. 

**Example:**

![Polystyrene](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/10/Polystyrene_formation.PNG)

In this case, the repeating units are also called the *monomer units*. 

Macromolecule
: A molecule of high relative molecular mass. 

Strictly speaking, a polymer is always a macromolecule but a macromolecule may not be a polymer.

**Example:**

A protein is not made by repeating one or two chemical units many times, but involves a precise selection from among 20 different amino acids. Thus a protein is a macromolecule but cannot be called a polymer by above definition. 

In reality, we always use polymer and macromolecule as interchangeable terms. The definition of a polymer has slightly changed from its original meaning. Actually, IUPAC uses macromolecule to define polymer. 

IUPAC definition of polymer
: A polymer is a substance composed of macromolecules. A macromolecule is a molecule of high relative molecular mass, the structure of which essentially comprises the multiple repetition of units derived, actually or conceptually, from molecules of low relative molecular mass.

**Polymerization:** is a process of reacting monomer molecules together in a chemical reaction to form polymers. 

**Molar Mass and Molecular Weight:** While these terms are often used interchangeably in polymer science, they have distinct formal definitions.
- **Molar Mass ($M$):** The mass of one mole of a substance, expressed in grams per mole (g/mol). A polymer typically has a molar mass $\geq 10^3$ g/mol.
- **Relative Molecular Mass ($M_r$), or Molecular Weight (M.W.):** A dimensionless quantity that is the ratio of the mass of a molecule to 1/12th the mass of a carbon-12 atom.
For simplicity, this course will follow the common convention in the field and use the term "molecular weight" when referring to molar mass.

**Oligomer:** A molecule with a small number of repeating units (typically $<10$).

**Degree of Polymerization ($\text{DP}$):** The number of monomeric units in a polymer, oligomer, or macromolecule. It's calculated by dividing the molecular weight of the polymer by the molecular weight of the repeat unit.

$$\text{DP}=\frac{\text{Polymer M.W.}}{\text{Repeat Unit M.W.}}$$

## History of polymer science{{attrs[#blk-hyrabw8sknyz]}}

### Prehistory

People have been using polymers for ... years, such as cotton and natural rubber. But when we talk about science, it means at least some level of fundamental understanding. 

#### Semi-synthetic celluloid and cellulose acetate.

In 1830s, Henri Braconnol along with Christian and others, developed derivatives of the natural polymer cellulose, producing new, semi-synthetic materials, such as celluloid and cellulose acetate. 

Celluloids 
: a class of materials produced by mixing nitrocellulose and camphor, often with added dyes and other agents. 

![2D skeletal chemical structure of nitrocellulose.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/83/Nitrocellulose-2D-skeletal.png =300x)

Nitrocellulose-based plastics slightly predate celluloid. Collodion, invented in 1848 and used as a wound dressing and an emulsion for photographic plates, is dried to a celluloid like film.

Once much more common for its use as photographic film before the advent of safer methods, celluloid's common present-day uses are for manufacturing table tennis balls, musical instruments, combs, office equipment, fountain pen bodies, and guitar picks.

Cellulose acetate 
: refers to any acetate ester of cellulose, usually cellulose diacetate.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2e/Formation_of_Cellulose_acetate_V1.png =300x)

In 1865, French chemist Paul Schützenberger discovered that cellulose reacts with acetic anhydride to form cellulose acetate. The German chemists Arthur Eichengrün and Theodore Becker invented the first soluble forms of cellulose acetate in 1903.

A bioplastic, cellulose acetate is used as a film base in photography, as a component in some coatings, and as a frame material for eyeglasses; it is also used as a synthetic fiber in the manufacture of cigarette filters and playing cards. In photographic film, cellulose acetate film replaced nitrate film in the 1950s, being far less flammable and cheaper to produce.

#### Vulcanized natural rubber

In the 1840s, Friedrich Ludersdorf and Nathaniel Hayward independently discovered that adding sulfur to raw natural rubber (polyisoprene) helped prevent the material from becoming sticky. In 1844 Charles Goodyear received a U.S. patent for vulcanizing natural rubber with sulfur and heat. Thomas Hancock had received a patent for the same process in the UK the year before. This process strengthened natural rubber and prevented it from melting with heat without losing flexibility. This made practical products such as waterproofed articles possible. It also facilitated practical manufacture of such rubberized materials. Vulcanized rubber represents the first commercially successful product of polymer research.

Natural rubber is polyisoprene that comes from tree syrup. After adding of sulfur, it becomes non sticky elastomer.

#### Bakelite

[Bakelite:](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bakelite)
A thermosetting [phenol formaldehyde resin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenol_formaldehyde_resin), formed from a condensation reaction of phenol with formaldehyde. 

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/95/3-D_Structure_of_Bakelite.png =300x)

Bakelite is the first plastic made from synthetic components. It was produced for the first time in 1872 by Adolf von Baeyer, though its use as a commercial product was not considered at the time. It was further developed by Leo Baekeland in Yonkers, New York, in 1907, and patented on December 7, 1909.

### Dawn of polymer science

![Hermann Staudinger](https://www.nobelprize.org/images/staudinger-13089-portrait-mini-2x.jpg =300x) 

The Nobel Prize in Chemistry 1953 was awarded to Hermann Staudinger "for his discoveries in the field of macromolecular chemistry".  In 1861, Thomas Graham that cellulose and other polymers were colloids, aggregates of molecules having small molecular mass connected by an unknown intermolecular force. Hermann Staudinger proposed in a landmark paper published in 1920 that rubber and other polymers such as starch, cellulose and proteins are long chains of short repeating molecular units linked by covalent bonds. It took over a decade for Staudinger's work to gain wide acceptance in the scientific community.

## Typical size of a polymer{{attrs[#blk-y9kbd5vrzky8]}}

### Molar Mass

As defined earlier, polymers are macromolecules with high molar mass, typically at least $10^3$ g/mol. For comparison, methane ($\ce{CH4}$) has a molar mass of 16.04 g/mol and a molecular diameter of $\pu{0.68 nm}$.

Molecules with a small number of repeating units (e.g., < 10) are called oligomers.

### Spatial Extent

Consider a linear polyethylene which has $\ce{-CH2-CH2 -}$ as the repeating units. 
- Each $\ce{C-C}$ bond has a fixed bond length of 0.153 nm. 
- Any two adjacent $\ce{C-C}$ bonds have a fixed bond angle of 109.5 $^\circ$. 
- The molecule can rotate around any $\ce{C-C}$ bond. 
- Fully extended polyethylene should have a zigzag profile. 

For a fully extended chain, the distance between every other $\ce{C}$ is 0.25 nm. If a vinyl polymer has a degree of polymerization $10^4$, the full length of the chain is $\pu{2.2 \mu m}$. 

Because the polymer chain is flexible, the actual size of a polymer chain increases as $N^{1/2}$. The spatial structure of a polymer chain is called the conformation.
