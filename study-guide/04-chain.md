## Methods and products{{attrs[#blk-n92d9d05lsnc]}}

A chain reaction describes a whole series of successive events triggered by some initial occurrence. 

Two major catagories of chain-growth polymerization: 

1. Polymerization of vinyl monomers (such as styrene and vinyl chloride) or monomers with substituted vinyl groups (such as methyl methacrylate and tetrafluoroethylene); 
2. Ring-opening polymerization of cyclic monomers (such as caprolactam and epoxide). 

For simplicity, in the rest of this document, when vinyl monomers are refered, monomers with both vinyl groups and substituted vinly groups are included. There are a large variety of vinyl monomers, some representative ones are shown below. 

![](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/wangyu16/PolymerScienceEducation/master/Fig/Vinyl-monomers.svg)

The carbon-carbon double bond is typically reactive. In organic chemistry, we learned about several types of addition reactions that can occur to a carbon-carbon double bond. One such reaction is nucleophilic addition, which results in a negatively charged carbanion as an intermediate product. With appropriate substituents, this carbanion can attack another molecule with a carbon-carbon double bond, i.e., a vinyl monomer, leading to a chain addition. This chain reaction, known as anionic polymerization, can be used to produce polymers. Addition reactions can also follow radical or cationic pathways, resulting in radical polymerization and cationic polymerization, respectively. Additionally, some organometallic species can trigger a coordination, insertion, and addition process, leading to coordination polymerization. Many vinyl monomers can undergo one or several types of polymerizations described above.  Consequently, many commercially useful products can be produced through these processes. The backbones of resulting polymers consist exclusively $\ce{-C-C -}$ bonds.

Ring-opening polymerization can proceed via anionic, or cationic mechanisms, or with the assistance of organometallic catalysts. Radical mechanism is also possible, though not very common. This type of polymerization is particularly useful for producing polymers with heteroatoms in the backbone, such as oxygen, nitrogen, or sulfur, which can impart unique properties to the resulting materials. 

### Radical polymerization

#### Properties of free radicals and features of radical polymerization

Free radicals are very active species which can potentially react with all components in the reaction system, such as the monomer, the polymer, the solvent, etc. Free radicals can also react one another resulting in stable species. Thus, the life-time of radicals is usually just a few seconds. However, this short time period is sufficient to lead to long chain molecules with thousands of monomer units or even above. 

One important feature of radicals is that they are electrically neutral and typically do not behave as either nucleophile or electrophile, thus radical polymerization can be carried out even in water regardless the medium is neutral, acidic or basic. Radical polymerization is not sensitive to many functional groups as well. Ionic polymerizations, on the other hand, are sensitive to tiny amount of moisture or functional groups prone to nucleophilic or electrophilic attacks. 

Radical polymerization is one of the easiest polymerization reactions to be carried out. Generally, small amount of impurities in the monomer, a little oxygen, and the presence of water do not prevent the formation of high molecular weight polymers. With sufficient amount of initiator and proper temperature (usually $\pu{50 ^\circ C}$ to $\pu{100 ^\circ C}$), the polymerization can reach high monomer conversion in one or a few hours. 

#### Available monomers  

Majority of vinyl monomers can be polymerized via radical polymerization. Exceptions include isobutylene, propylene, and vinyl ether. Monomers with functional groups such as carboxylic acid, hydroxyl, and amino can also be polymerized directly via radical polymerization.  

#### Reaction techniques

**Solution polymerization** 

Solution reactions are typically the most convenient method for performing organic synthesis, including polymerizations. The presence of a solvent helps maintain the polymerization system as a fluid rather than a solid mass, making it easier to remove the product from the reactor. If the resulting polymer is water-soluble, the polymerization can be carried out in an aqueous solution. Otherwise, many organic solvents can be used. However, some commonly used organic solvents, such as chloroform, may react rapidly with radicals and should be avoided 

**Bulk polymerization**

In solution polymerization, removing the solvent after polymerization is essential. To reuse the solvent, further purification is required. Both incur additional costs. In some cases, avoiding the use of solvents altogether is advisable if possible. 

Bulk polymerization involves only the monomer and initiator as starting agents. Commercial examples of bulk radical polymerization are limited due to heat dissipation and viscosity issues. However, for products like flat plastic plates or sheets, bulk polymerization is convenient.

Polymethyl methacrylate (PMMA) is a highly transparent and rigid plastic that can replace glass. To make PMMA plates, the monomer and initiator are polymerized to a low conversion to form a syrup, which is essentially a solution of PMMA in its own monomers. This viscous syrup is cast into a mold and further polymerized to consume all the monomers, resulting in a solidified product.

During this process, it is crucial to avoid air bubbles. Additionally, the expansion during polymerization and shrinkage upon cooling must be considered. 

**Suspension polymerization** 

In contrast to solution polymerization, if you choose a solvent that is immiscible with the monomer and does not dissolve the polymer, a suspension system can be created under mechanical agitation. The most commonly used suspension system involves water and non-water-soluble monomers. Polymerization under these conditions offers several advantages over solution polymerization:

1. The monomer forms small droplets in the suspension system and eventually becomes polymer particles. The viscosity of the system remains low since the polymers are solid particles.  
2. Heat dissipation is easier than in solution polymerization.  
3. Removal of water by simple filtration is possible.

**Emulsion polymerization**

Emulsions can be created by adding surfactants to immiscible monomers and solvents. In this case, mild stirring is sufficient to prevent phase separation. Polymerization can be carried out in emulsions, which offer all the advantages of suspension polymerization. The monomer droplets and polymer particles, typically 50–500 nm, are even smaller than in suspension polymerization. Radicals are often separated in individual monomer droplets, resulting in longer lifetimes and higher molecular weights compared to other radical polymerization techniques.

However, the addition of surfactants raises the question of how to remove them after polymerization. Additionally, since the polymer particles are much smaller than in suspension polymerization, separating the polymers from water is more challenging. In many cases, when using emulsion polymerization, the resulting mixture is used directly without further separation. The latex, i.e., the dispersion resulting from emulsion polymerization, is used directly in adhesives, paints, paper coatings, and textile coatings. These are often preferred over solvent-based products due to the absence of volatile organic compounds.

#### Products

**Low density polyethylene (LDPE)** 

Ethylene can be polymerized via a radical mechanism. However, ethylene is a gas at ambient conditions and has relatively low reactivity towards radical addition. Therefore, high pressure, e.g., 1200 -- 3000 bar, is required for the radical polymerization of ethylene. During this polymerization process, the propagating radicals can also react with already-formed polymer chains, causing radical transfer from the chain end to the mid-chain and initiating branches. As a result, radical polymerization of ethylene under high pressure produces branched polyethylene, which has relatively low crystallinity and low density.

**Polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE)**

Tetrafluoroethylene is ethylene with the four hydrogen atoms replaced by fluorine. PTFE is produced by free-radical polymerization of tetrafluoroethylene. The molecular weights of the polymers are high, ranging up to $10^5 - 10^6$, due to the lack of appreciable chain transfer reactions and the precipitation of growing radicals leading to greatly decreased termination.

### Anionic polymerization 

#### Properties of carbanions and features of anionic polymerization

A carbanion is the conjugate base of a carbon acid:

$$\ce{R3CH + B− → R3C− + HB}$$

where B stands for the base.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/34/Carbanion_Structural_Formulae_V.1.svg =300x)

Usually, only systems with high purity monomers and aprotic solvent can lead to sufficient polymerization. Monomers with proton donating functional groups apparently will not work. Tiny amount of moisture can affect the polymerization.

### Cationic polymerization

#### Properties of carbocations and features of cationic polymerization

A carbocation is any even-electron cation with significant partial positive charge on a carbon atom. In polymerization, the carbocation is formed by protonation of alkene resulting in a formal positive charge that is surrounded by a sextet of electrons instead of the usual octet required to fill the valence shell of carbon. Therefore, carbenium ions (and carbocations in general) are often reactive, seeking to fill the octet of valence electrons as well as regain a neutral charge. The protonation is a reversible process. When the deprotonation happens, a chain end double bond will be formed and the proton would transfer to the counterion or another monomer.

### Initiation

One key feature of chain-growth polymerization is that monomers do not react directly with each other. Instead, a specific type of active center must be introduced into the polymerization system. This active center reacts with the monomers. After the addition of one monomer, the active center links to the monomer, generating a new active center of the same type on the opposite end of the newly attached monomer unit. 

The addition of the first monomer units to either externally introduced or in-situ generated active centers is known as initiation.

#### Acids for cationic polymerization

During initiation, a carbenium ion is generated from which the polymer chain is made. The counterion should be non-nucleophilic, otherwise the reaction is terminated instantaneously.

Upon reaction of the initiator with the coinitiator, an intermediate complex is formed which then goes on to react with the monomer unit. The counterion produced by the initiator-coinitiator complex is less nucleophilic than that of the Brønsted acid $\ce{A−}$ counterion. ### Propagation

Propagation is the process in which the active centers react with monomers repeatedly to form long chain molecules. Typically, the reaction rate is proportional to the concentrations of both the active centers and the monomers. 

$$R_p = k_p[\ce{P}^*][\ce{M}]$$

where $\ce{P}^*$ is the propogating polymer chain with an active species at the chain end and $\ce{M}$ is the monomer.  

Temperature certainly plays a role on the propagation rate. Solvent can also affect the rate coefficient. In case of radical polymerization, the effect of solvent is relatively weak; while in case of ionic polymerizations, choice of solvents could be critical. The rate of ionic polymerization is also significantly influenced by the counterions.

The reaction mechanisms are discussed in the "Methods and products" section.

#### Concentration of active centers

Active centers could be relatively stable under polymerization conditions and keep nearly constant concentrations until termination reagents are added on purpose. For example, in anionic polymerization, the number of propagating anions nearly do not change even after monomers are completely consumed. 

In some other cases, the transfer of active centers happens frequently but the total concentration keeps nearly constant. Cationic polymerization could be in this category. 

The other scenario is that the generation and consumption of the active center reaches a dynamic steady state that the concentration also maintains at a constant level in a relatively long time period. The example is radical polymerization. The radical concentration can be calculated by assuming the rate of initiation equals that of termination. This is called the **steady state assumption**.

$$
\begin{align*}
& R_i = 2k_df[\ce{I}] \\
& R_t = 2k_t[\ce{P*}]^2 \\
& 2k_df[\ce{I}] = 2k_t[\ce{P*}]^2\\
& [\ce{P*}] = \left(\frac{k_d[\ce{I}]f}{k_t}\right)^{1/2}
\end{align*}
$$

#### Propagation rate coefficient

Different monomers could have dramatically different propagation rate coefficient. In case of radical polymerization, the relative reactivity of monomers determines what kind of copolymer would be produced when copolymerization of these monomers is carried out. 

In case of ionic polymerization, the reactivity of the active centers varies dramatically with the structure of the monomer. Usually, a less reactive propagating ion cannot add to a monomer and generate a more reactive propagating ion. Thus copolymerization is more challenging. 

#### Trommsdorff–Norrish effect 

In radical polymerization there is a common phenomenon where the rate of a radical polymerization reaction suddenly increases during the later stages of the reaction. Such phenomenon is called **self-acceleration** or **Trommsdorff-Norrish effect**. The reason lies in the increase of viscosity and the entanglement of polymer chains that they cannot easily collide and terminate, leading to a rapid acceleration in polymerization rate. 

### Termination

Termination can occur spontaneously among active centers, as seen in radical polymerization, or between propagating chains and externally added reagents. The result of termination is polymers with stable chain ends that no longer propagate.  

In case of radical polymerization, the radicals can react with each other by either coupling or disproportionation. Both reactions cause termination of propagating chains. The rate coefficient is typically diffusion controlled because of the high activity of radicals that they react with each other once encounter with each other. 

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b0/Termination_-_combination.png =400x)
![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b1/Termination_-_disproportionation.png =400x)

Ionic polymerizations do not have direct termination between two propagating chains because the same type of chain end ions repulse each other. 

### Competing reactions towards propagation

In an ideal scenario, active centers react exclusively with monomers according to the propagation reaction mechanism. However, due to the high reactivity of the active centers, many competing reactions often occur. These competing reactions add complexity to the reaction system, leading to undesired products. Nonetheless, they can also offer opportunities for tailoring the molecular architecture or controlling the chain length and functionality, which will be discussed in "Advanced polymerization methods".  

Chain transfer
: In polymer chemistry, chain transfer is a polymerization reaction by which the activity of a growing polymer chain is transferred to another molecule

Chain transfer reactions occur in most forms of addition polymerization including radical polymerization, ring-opening polymerization, coordination polymerization, and cationic polymerization. Anionic polymerizations sometimes have extremely low level of chain transfer. 

The high-pressure radical polymerization of ethylene, the method used to produce low-density polyethylene (LDPE), is characterized by significant branching due to prevalent chain transfer to polymer. In the radical polymerization of vinyl chloride, chain transfer to monomer is substantial, making it the primary factor regulating polymer molecular weight, rather than termination.

### Average degree of polymerization

**Instantaneous average degree of polymerization**

The average degree of polymerization ($DP$) can be calculated by comparing the rate of propagation to the rate of all reactions that can potentially terminate the propagation process. In case there is no termination reactions, the result will be infinity. The actual $DP$ cannot be infinity, the calculation simply imply that the polymers have the potential to continue growing to infinity length. When there are reactions to stop propagation, this method defines the instantaneous average $DP$, i.e., the average $DP$ of polymers produced at a specific moment. 

Due to the change of concentrations and viscosity, the reaction rates also vary during the polymerization. Thus, the polymers produced at the beginning of the reaction may have dramatically different lengths compared to the polymers produced at high monomer conversion. 

**Overall number average degree of polymerization**

The overall number average degree of polymerization can be calculated as the number of monomers have been consumed versus the number of polymers produced during the reaction. Since the number of monomers can be calculated easily based on the conversion, the critical question is what determines the number of polymers. 

In a simplest case, one initiator create one active center which grow into one polymer chain. The number of polymers equals the number of initiators. Some anionic polymerization follow such a simple senario. 

Radical polymerizations with thermal initiators are also relatively simple, if chain transfer reactions are not significant. Each initiator usually produce two primary radicals and result in one or two polymers. Remember to take into account the initiation efficiency, the calculated average $DP$ should not be far from the experimental value. 

$$DP_n = \frac{k_p[\ce{M}]}{2k_t[\ce{P*}]} = \frac{k_p[\ce{M}]}{2(fk_tk_d[\ce{I}])^{1/2}}$$

When chain transfer or other competing reactions are significant, the situation becomes more complicated and sometimes it is very difficult to estimate the average $DP$.


### Molecular weight distribution

Consider two systems.  
1. Anionic polymerization with constant concentration of active centers, no termination, nor chain transfer. 
2. Radical polymerization with initiation and termination reaches a stady state without significant chain transfer reactions. 

#### Anionic polymerization

The number fraction distribution function (the probability that a given chain has degree of polymerization *i*) is given by the **Poisson distribution** function:

$$x(i)=\frac{(DP_n-1)^{i-1}}{(i-1)!}\exp(1-DP_n)$$

where $i$ is an integer, i.e., 1, 2, 3, ...; $DP_n = \sum ix(i)$ is the number average degree of polymerization; $x(i)$ is the number fraction of $i$-mers in the system. 

The weight fraction distribution function is determined following equation:

$$w(i)=\frac{ix(i)}{\sum ix(i)}=\frac{i(DP_n-1)^{i-1}}{DP_n(i-1)!}\exp(1-DP_n)$$

and 

$$DP_w = \sum iw(i)$$

The dispersity for the Poisson distribution is :

$$\frac{DP_w}{DP_n}=1+\frac{1}{DP_n}-\frac{1}{DP_n^2}\approx 1 + \frac{1}{DP_n}$$

This applies for ideal living polymerization. It indicates the distribution becomes narrower with increasing $DP$. The chain lengths get more uniform as $DP$ increases. 

:::success 
The process of anionic polymerization can be represented by a simple statistical model: $N$ balls are randomly thrown into $n$ boxes, where $N \gg n$. We want to find the probability, $x(i)$, that a specific box contains exactly $i$ balls. This scenario perfectly describes a binomial distribution, but because $N \gg n$, we can approximate the binomial distribution with a Poisson distribution.

- For a single box, the probability of a ball landing in that box is $p = 1/n$.
- We have $N$ independent trials (each ball toss).
- The probability of exactly $i$ balls landing in that box is given by the binomial probability:
$$x(i) = P(X = i) = (N \text{ choose } i) \times p^i \times (1-p)^{(N-i)}$$
where $(N \text{ choose } i) = N! / (i! \times (N-i)!)$

Poisson Approximation:

When $N$ is large and $p$ is small, the binomial distribution can be approximated by a Poisson distribution.

- The Poisson distribution has a single parameter, $\lambda$, which is the average number of events (in our case, balls in a box).
- $\lambda = N \times p = N / n$ (the average number of balls per box, i.e., number average degree of polymerization $DP_n$).
- The Poisson probability mass function is:
$$x(i) = \frac{\lambda^i}{i!}e^{-\lambda} $$

In this case, the value of $i$ could be 0, 1, 2, 3, .... In an actual polymerization system, the minimal value of $i$ is 1. 
:::

#### Radical polymerization 

A propagating radical can undergo propagation or termination, while chain transfer is omitted. The probability of propagation versus propagation plus termination is defined as $p$ which resembles the extent of reaction in step-growth polymerization. If the termination is only by disproportionation, the probability to find an $i$-mer is 

$$x(i) = p^{i-1}(1-p)$$

This also has the same format as in step-growth polymerization. Thus,

$$\frac{DP_w}{DP_n} = 1+p \approx 2$$

In case of termination by coupling, the mathematics is a little more complicated. The result is

$$\frac{DP_w}{DP_n} \approx 1.5$$

Note that the value of $p$ changes during polymerization, the average chain lengthes of polymers produced at different time could be dramatically different. The overall $\frac{DP_w}{DP_n}$ value could be much greater than 2.

## Copolymerization{{attrs[#blk-qdz2x337f0zn]}}

A copolymer is a polymer derived from more than one species of monomer. Copolymers can be produced by concurrent polymerization of more than one type of monomers, or by sequential polymerization, or by post-polymerization reactions. Since a copolymer consists of at least two types of constituent units (also structural units), copolymers can be classified based on how these units are arranged along the chain. Linear copolymers consist of a single main chain and include alternating copolymers, statistical copolymers, and block copolymers. Branched copolymers consist of a single main chain with one or more polymeric side chains, and can be grafted, star shaped, or have other architectures.

The concurrent polymerization of different types of monomers into copolymers is called copolymerization. In principle, such copolymerization should provide statistical linear copolymer. However, the actual result is more complicated. 

### Reactivity ratio

Recall that the rate of propagation in chain growth polymerization is proportional to the concentrations of both the active centers and the monomers. In the case of copolymerization involving two monomers, four concentrations must be considered: the two types of monomers and the two types of active centers derived from each kind of monomer unit. We assume that only the last monomer unit affects the activity of the active center, which, while not entirely accurate, serves as a reasonable simplification.

In addition to the concentrations, the reaction rate also depends on the rate coefficient, which reflects the activities of the reacting species. With two types of active centers and two types of monomers, there are four possible pairs, each with its own rate coefficient. Therefore, four rate coefficients must be taken into account. 

Consider two monomers, labeled as Monomer 1 and Monomer 2. The active centers derived from each monomer are also labeled as 1 and 2, respectively. The rate coefficients for the interactions between these monomers and active centers are shown in the table below:

|                 | Monomer 1 | Monomer 2 |
| --------------- | --------- | --------- |
| Active center 1 | $k_{11}$  | $k_{12}$  |
| Active center 2 | $k_{21}$  | $k_{22}$  |

$$\ce{-M1* + M1 ->[k_{11}] -M1M1*}$$
$$\ce{-M1* + M2 ->[k_{12}] -M1M2*}$$
$$\ce{-M2* + M1 ->[k_{21}] -M2M1*}$$
$$\ce{-M2* + M2 ->[k_{22}] -M2M2*}$$

The four reaction rates are

$$R_{p,11} = k_{11}[\ce{M1*}][\ce{M1}]$$
$$R_{p,12} = k_{12}[\ce{M1*}][\ce{M2}]$$
$$R_{p,21} = k_{21}[\ce{M2*}][\ce{M1}]$$
$$R_{p,22} = k_{22}[\ce{M2*}][\ce{M2}]$$

We are interested in the distribution of both monomer units in the resulting polymers. To describe this, we define two reactivity ratios:

$$r_1 = \frac{k_{11}}{k_{12}}\quad\text{and}\quad r_2 = \frac{k_{22}}{k_{21}}$$

These ratios represent the probability of a given active center adding a monomer of the same type versus a different type.

### Product composition

Consider the ratio of the instantenous consumption of each monomer that equals the ratio of composition of the copolymer formed at that moment. The consumption of each monomer can be expressed by:

$$-\frac{\mathrm{d}[\ce{M1}]}{\mathrm{d}t} = k_{11}[\ce{M1*}][\ce{M1}] + k_{21}[\ce{M2*}][\ce{M1}]$$
$$-\frac{\mathrm{d}[\ce{M2}]}{\mathrm{d}t} = k_{12}[\ce{M1*}][\ce{M2}] + k_{22}[\ce{M2*}][\ce{M2}]$$

Thus,

$$\frac{\mathrm{d}[\ce{M1}]}{\mathrm{d}[\ce{M2}]} = \frac{k_{11}[\ce{M1*}][\ce{M1}] + k_{21}[\ce{M2*}][\ce{M1}]}{k_{12}[\ce{M1*}][\ce{M2}] + k_{22}[\ce{M2*}][\ce{M2}]}$$

Assume the concentration of both types of active centers are constant know as the steady-state approximation. As a result, the rate of crossover between the different types of terminal active centers must be equal, i.e., $R_{p,12} = R_{p,21}$.

$$k_{12}[\ce{M1*}][\ce{M2}] = k_{21}[\ce{M2*}][\ce{M1}]$$

thus, 

$$\frac{[\ce{M1*}]}{[\ce{M2*}]} = \frac{k_{21}[\ce{M1}]}{k_{12}[\ce{M2}]}$$

Combining above equations, we get the copolymer composition equation:

$$\frac{\mathrm{d}[\ce{M1}]}{\mathrm{d}[\ce{M2}]} = \frac{[\ce{M1}]}{[\ce{M2}]}\frac{r_1[\ce{M1}] + [\ce{M2}]}{r_2[\ce{M2}] + [\ce{M1}]} = \frac{1+r_1[\ce{M1}]/[\ce{M2}]}{1+r_2[\ce{M2}]/[\ce{M1}]}$$

Define the feedstock fractions of monomers are $f_1$ and $f_2$, respectively. The composition fractions of monomer units in the polymer are $F_1$ and $F_2$. 

$$f_1 = 1-f_2 = \frac{[\ce{M1}]}{[\ce{M1}]+[\ce{M2}]}$$

$$F_1 = 1-F_2 = \frac{\mathrm{d}[\ce{M1}]}{\mathrm{d}[\ce{M1}]+\mathrm{d}[\ce{M2}]}$$

We have the correlation

$$F_1 = \frac{r_1f_1^2 + f_1f_2}{r_1f_1^2 + 2f_1f_2 + r_2f_2^2}$$

So far what we have discussed is valid for any type of chain-growth polymerization. However, radical polymerizaitonis the most extensively studied.
