## Architecture and composition{{attrs[#blk-eavllfq810gv]}}

>Throughout this course, always keep this question in mind: how does the structure affect the physical/mechanical property of the material?

The physical/mechanical properties of a polymer material often related to the molecular weight, molecular architecture, and the interaction among the monomer units of the molecule. 

edit

A few general trends are:
- Higher degree of crystalization results in stronger/harder material.
- Long range regular molecular structure enhance the ability to crystalize.
- Longer molecules make crystalization less efficient. 
- Longer molecules make the material tougher, which means more difficult to break. 
- Crystalline polymers are usually non transparent. 

>Use the same type of polymer, i.e., same monomer units, you can create a large variety of materials simply by changing the molecular weight and molecular structure.

#### Different types of polyethylenes (PE) 

![PE](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c5/Polyethylene-repeat-2D.png =150x)

- The particular material properties of "polyethylene" depend on its molecular structure.
- Molecular weight and crystallinity are the most significant factors.
- crystallinity in turn depends on molecular weight and degree of branching. 
 
The most commonly used PE products are HDPE and LDPE. There are also many other types of PE for special applications.  
 
1. **High density polyethylene (HDPE)** HDPE has mostly linear molecules that pack together well (crystalize), so intermolecular forces are stronger than in highly branched polymers. HDPE has high tensile strength. It is used in products and packaging such as milk jugs, detergent bottles, butter tubs, garbage containers, and water pipes.  
![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3e/PE-HD_schematic.svg)  
2. **Low density polyethylene (LDPE)** LDPE has a high degree of short- and long-chain branching, which means that the chains do not pack into the crystal structure as well. It has, weaker intermolecular forces. This results in a lower tensile strength and increased ductility. LDPE is used for both rigid containers and plastic film applications such as plastic bags and film wrap.  
![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8b/PE-LD_schematic.svg)  
3. **Ultra-high-molecular-weight (UHMWPE)** UHMWPE is polyethylene with a molecular weight numbering in the millions, usually between 3.5 and 7.5 million. Compared to regular HDPE, the higher molecular weight makes crystallization less efficient but the material is more difficult to break. Thus the product is more flexible yet tougher. UHMWPE is used in a diverse range of applications. These include can- and bottle-handling machine parts, moving parts on weaving machines, bearings, gears, artificial joints, edge protection on ice rinks, steel cable replacements on ships, and butchers' chopping boards. It is commonly used for the construction of articular portions of implants used for hip and knee replacements. As fiber, it competes with aramid in bulletproof vests. 
4. **Cross-linked polyethylene (PEX or XLPE)** PEX is a medium- to high-density polyethylene containing cross-link bonds introduced into the polymer structure, changing the thermoplastic into a thermoset. The high-temperature properties of the polymer are improved, its flow is reduced, and its chemical resistance is enhanced. PEX is used in some potable-water plumbing systems because tubes made of the material can be expanded to fit over a metal nipple and it will slowly return to its original shape, forming a permanent, water-tight connection.
5. **Linear low-density polyethylene (LLDPE)** LLDPE is a substantially linear polymer with significant numbers of short branches, commonly made by copolymerization of ethylene with short-chain alpha-olefins. LLDPE has higher tensile strength than LDPE, and it exhibits higher impact and puncture resistance than LDPE. Lower-thickness (gauge) films can be blown, compared with LDPE, with better environmental stress cracking resistance. LLDPE is used in packaging, particularly film for bags and sheets. Lower thickness may be used compared to LDPE. It is used for cable coverings, toys, lids, buckets, containers, and pipe. While other applications are available, LLDPE is used predominantly in film applications due to its toughness, flexibility, and relative transparency. Product examples range from agricultural films, Saran wrap, and bubble wrap to multilayer and composite films.

#### Sequence isomerism

In a regular vinyl polymer all monomer units are normally linked in a head to tail configuration so that all β-substituents are separated by three carbon atoms. In head to head configuration this separation is only by two carbon atoms and the separation with tail to tail configuration is by four atoms. The difference seems trivial, but can significantly affect the chemical property of the polymer. One example is poly(vinyl alcohol).

### Copolymer

Homopolymer
: A polymer made up of many copies of a single repeating unit, or monomer. 

Copolymer
: A polymer that is made up of two or more monomer species, i.e., different structural units exist in one macromolecule.

Polymer blend
: A member of a class of materials analogous to metal alloys, in which at least two polymers are blended together to create a new material with different physical properties.

Copolymers and polymer blends are employed to create new materials with novel properties. 

#### Statistical copolymer

Statistical copolymer
: is a polymer molecule made up of two or more different monomers that are arranged in a random sequence without a specific order. 

Statistical copolymers are also called random copolymers. 

Example and practice question: What properties does a propylene and ethylene copolymer have? Is it rigid or flexible? Transparent or translucent? What applications is it suitable for?

Example: ABS is a terpolymer made by polymerizing styrene and acrylonitrile in the presence of polybutadiene. The proportions can vary from 15% to 35% acrylonitrile, 5% to 30% butadiene and 40% to 60% styrene. The result is a long chain of polybutadiene crisscrossed with shorter chains of poly(styrene-co-acrylonitrile). The nitrile groups from neighboring chains, being polar, attract each other and bind the chains together, making ABS stronger than pure polystyrene. The acrylonitrile also contributes chemical resistance, fatigue resistance, hardness, and rigidity, while increasing the heat deflection temperature. The styrene gives the plastic a shiny, impervious surface, as well as hardness, rigidity, and improved processing ease. The polybutadiene, a rubbery substance, provides toughness and ductility at low temperatures, at the cost of heat resistance and rigidity.

#### Alternating copolymer

An alternating copolymer has regular alternating A and B units, and is often described by the formula: -A-B-A-B-A-B-A-B-A-B-, or -(-A-B-)n-. The molar ratio of each monomer in the polymer is normally close to one. Synthetically it is interesting, but essentially the polymer is as if a homopolymer with -AB- as the repeating units. 

Can a typical step-growth polymerization of AA and BB monomers be considered as alternating copolymerization? For example, the production of Nylon6,6. 

Sometimes, alternating copolymerization can produce interesting polymers, such as radical polymerization of styrene and maleic anhydride.

## Molecular weight distribution{{attrs[#blk-zdzqukd82mhv]}}

Synthetic polymer samples consist of molecules with varying chain lengths due to the statistical nature of chain growth during polymerization. When we talk about the molecular weight of polymers, it is usually an average molecular weight of some sort.

### Average molecular weight

Suppose we have a polymer sample containing molecules with different degrees of polymerization. 
- The molecules with the degree of polymerization $i$ are called $i$-mers. 
- If the molecular weight of a monomer unit is $M_0$, the molecular weight of a $i$-mer should be $iM_0$. 
- The number of $i$-mers in the sample is $n_i$, thus the total number of molecules should be $\sum_i n_i$. 

**Number average**

The definition of number average molecular weight is straightforward, i.e., the sum of the molecular weight of all molecules in the sample devided by the totoal number of molecules.

$$
\begin{equation*}
M_n = \frac{\sum_i n_iM_i}{\sum_i n_i}
\end{equation*}
$$

We can also calculate the number average molecular weight from another approach by asking "What is the number fraction of $i$-mers in the sample?" The answer is simple

$$x_i = \frac{n_i}{\sum_i n_i}$$

The mean value of molecular weight can be calculated as

$$M_n = \sum_i x_iM_i$$

**Weight average**

If we ask "What is the weight fraction of $i$-mers?" The answer is the weight of all $i$-mers devided by the weight of the whole sample.

$$w_i = \frac{n_iM_i}{\sum_i n_iM_i}$$

The weight average molecular weight can be defined as the mean value of molecular weight based on the weight fractions. 

$$
\begin{align*}
M_w & = \sum_i w_iM_i \\ 
& = \sum_i\frac{n_iM_i^2}{\sum_i n_iM_i} \\
& = \frac{\sum_i n_iM_i^2}{\sum_i n_iM_i}
\end{align*}
$$

**$z$-average**

The formulas of number and weight average molecular weight can be written in a general format

$$M_x = \frac{\sum_i n_iM_i^x}{\sum_i n_iM_i^{x-1}}$$

When $x=1$, the formula gives number average molecular weight; when $x=2$, it gives the weight average. We can define higher order average molecular weight simply by increase the value of $x$. For example, $z$-average molecular weight can be defined as

$$M_z = \frac{\sum_i n_iM_i^3}{\sum_i n_iM_i^2}$$

$M_z$ is not directly related to a simple fraction like $x_i$ or $w_i$, it does have some experimental relevance. 

What is the meaning of the higher order average molecular weights?  There is a direct relationship between these values and the moments of the distribution of the molecular weight. 

### Calculate average molecular weight using moments

**Moment** is an important concept in statistics. Since molecular weight has a discrete distribution, the $n$-th moment is calculated as the follows

$$Mn = \sum_i M_i^n\times x_i$$

where $n$ is an integer equals 0, 1, 2, 3, .... $M_i$ and $x_i$ are the molecular weight and the number fraction of the $i$-th type of ingredient in the sample. 

Thus, the 0th moment should equal to 1 since it is the sum of the number fraction of all ingredients. The 1st moment equals to the number average molecular weight. The weight average molecular weight equals $M2/M1$ and the $z$-average equals $M3/M2$. The higher order average molecular weight can also be calculated accordingly. 


### Distribution of molecular weight

Even if the number average molecular weights are the same, two polymer samples could have dramatically different properties if the molecular weight distributions are different. To know the full profile of the molecular weight distribution is not easy, we should at least know how broad the distribution is to have better evaluation of the properties of the polymer sample. 

We know the number average molecular weight is the mean value of the molecular weight regarding the number fractions of each degree of polymerization. We would like to know the standard deviation of the molecular weight. 

Standard deviation is defined as 

$$\sigma = \left[\sum_i x_i(M_i - M_n)^2\right]^{1/2}$$

where 
- $(M_i - M_n)^2$ means the square of the deviation from the mean value;
- the sum with a $x_i$ factor means to take mean value of that. 

We can rearrange the equation

$$
\begin{align*}
\sigma & = \left[\sum_i x_i(M_i - M_n)^2\right]^{1/2} \\
& = \left[\sum_i x_i (M_i^2 -2M_iM_n + M_n^2)\right]^{1/2} \\
& = \left[\sum_ix_iM_i^2 - 2M_n\sum_ix_iM_i + M_n^2\sum_ix_i\right]^{1/2} \\
& =\left[\sum_ix_iM_i^2 - M_n^2\right]^{1/2} \\
& = M_n\left[\frac{\sum_ix_iM_i^2}{M_n\sum_ix_iM_i} - 1\right]^{1/2}
\end{align*}
$$

In the last step we used $M_n = \sum_ix_iM_i$. 

The weight average molecular weight is

$$M_w = \frac{\sum_in_iM_i^2}{\sum_in_iM_i} = \frac{\sum_ix_iM_i^2}{\sum_ix_iM_i}$$

Thus, the above expression of $\sigma$ can be written as

$$\sigma = M_n\left[\frac{M_w}{M_n} -  1\right]^{1/2}$$

In polymer science, people usually use the value $M_w/M_n$ as an indicator of the distribution of the molecular weight. This makes sense because the value

$$\left[\frac{M_w}{M_n} - 1\right]^{1/2}$$

tells the root mean square deviation of the molecular weights relative to the average value, i.e., $\sigma/M_n$. 

If the value of $M_w/M_n$ is 1.5, and $M_n$ is 10000, we can tell that the root mean square deviation of the molecular weight is about $\sqrt{1.5-1} = 0.7$ times $M_n$.

## Origin and destiny of polymers{{attrs[#blk-zcblrzi2qf51]}}

Synthetic polymers: The man-made polymers or the polymers which are synthesized in the laboratory are called synthetic polymers. Synthetic polymers find a large application in our everyday life. Most of the things that we use are made up of synthetic polymers. Some of the typical examples of synthetic polymers are polyethylene, polyvinyl chloride, Bakelite, nylon, synthetic rubbers.

Natural polymers: The polymers which are obtained naturally are called natural polymers. A natural polymer has its origin in plants and animals. Starch, cellulose, proteins, natural rubber etc. are the examples of natural polymers.

There are two primary categories of polymerization methods: step-growth polymerization and chain-growth polymerization. Each category encompasses various reaction mechanisms, which will be discussed in detail in the subsequent chapters.

### Step growth polymerization

Step-growth polymerization
: refers to a type of polymerization mechanism in which bi-functional or multifunctional monomers react to form first dimers, then trimers, longer oligomers and eventually long chain polymers.

- All monomers and polymers in the system have reactive functional groups. 
- Usually, each reaction brings two molecules together to become one. Each reaction reduces the number of molecules by one. 
- Intermolecular reaction could happen. In this case, the reaction will not change the total number of molecules. 

In principle, any reaction that can link two molecular units, say A and B, together giving AB could be used to make polymers. However, in reality, only a few organic reactions are used to make commercial polymer products. Why? What reactions that you learned in organic chemistry can be used to produce useful polymers? 

Condensation polymerization
: is a type of step-growth polymerization in which small molecules are produced and removed from the reaction system while the rest . 

For example, difunctional alcohols and difunctional carboxylic acids react to make polyester and water is produced as the by product.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/75/Polyester_formation_via_direct_esterification.svg)

Polyaddition
: is a type of step-growth polymerization in which two molecules link together 

For example, difunctional alcohols and difunctional isocyanates react to produce polyurethane. 

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/ca/Polyurethane.png =600x)


### Chain growth polymerization

Chain-growth polymerization 
: is a polymerization technique where monomer molecules add onto the active site on a growing polymer chain one at a time. There are a limited number of these active sites at any moment during the polymerization which gives this method its key characteristics.

Typically monomers do not react with each other directly. 

For example, polystyrene is produce by radical polymerization of styrene. The must be a radical source to initiate the polymerization. 

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bf/Styrene_radical_chain_polymerization.jpg)

Monomers for chain-growth polymerization typically have one functional group that can potentially lind to two other molecules and once activated it would result in a highly reactive species. 

Vinyl monomers are one big category of compounds that can undergo chain-growth polymerization. Such as styrene, once a radical attack and link to one side of the vinyl group, the other side of the vinyl group becomes a new radical that can react with another monomer. Besides radical addition, vinyl monomers may be polymerized via cationic, anionic or coordination addition reactions. 

Cyclic monomers are another big category of compounds suitable for chain-growth polymerization. Imagine an AB type of monomer which normally would be polymerized via step-growth polymerization to get polymers look like -ABABABABAB-. Now let the AB type of monomer react intermolecularly to form a small cyclic molecule. Then the cyclic molecule can be polymerized by opening the internel link and form new connections among one another. 

An example is polycaprolactone which is produced by the ring-opening polymerization of ε-caprolactone. In principle, the polymer can be obained by step-growth polymerization of 6-hydroxyhexanoic acid as well. But the former method is preferred for commercial production, why?

### Destinies of polymers

Naturally occurring polymers are typically decomposed by living organisms, such as microbes, into small molecules. These molecules can then be utilized by living organisms to produce new generations of naturally occurring polymers.

Fossil fuels are formed from the remains of ancient organisms, including animals, plants, and plankton, which were buried and transformed over millions of years. Ancient Earth likely had a higher quantity of surface carbon. If we were to unearth and burn large quantities of fossil fuels, releasing the carbon as $\ce{CO2}$ in a relatively short time period, say one or two hundred years, what would happen?

Synthetic polymers are primarily derived from fossil fuels. What are their final destinies, and how do they compare to other types of man-made materials?

In general, inorganic materials such as glass, metal alloys, and metal oxides create fewer environmental concerns. However, exceptions include nuclear fuels, which are naturally occurring radioactive elements that have been collected and concentrated. When released into the environment due to accidents, they can cause disasters, such as the Chernobyl and Fukushima nuclear accidents.

Many organic compounds can have significant environmental impacts. For example, DDT (dichlorodiphenyltrichloroethane), a chemical compound developed in the 1940s as an insecticide, and more recently, the herbicide Roundup (isopropylamine salt of glyphosate). In recent years, PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances) have frequently made headlines due to their environmental and health concerns.

Many polymers have a common problem: they are environmentally persistent. Though this is also the property what we need for many applications. 

#### Conventional ways to deal with polymer wastes

Final destinies of synthetic polymers include: 

1. Landfills. A significant portion of synthetic polymers ends up in landfills, where they can take hundreds to thousands of years to decompose. If there is no leakage of harmful chemicals from the landfill sites, this is not too bad. 
2. Incineration. Some synthetic polymers are incinerated for energy recovery. While this reduces waste volume, it can release harmful pollutants and greenhouse gases if not managed properly.
3. Recycling. Efforts to recycle synthetic polymers are increasing, but challenges remain due to the complexity of sorting and processing different types of plastics. Importantly, recycled plastics usually have inferier mechanical/physical properties. 

Besides, many plastic wastes are not collected properly, leaving pacific garbage patch and microplastic issues. The key problem is they are persistence in the environment. There is not enough time for the nature, e.g., microbes, to learn how to decomposite synthetic polymers efficiently. 

#### Fugure directions

To deal with these problems, we need better solution for degradation or depolymerization of synthetic polymers. Generally, the next generation of materials should follow the rule of recyclable by design. Ideally, everything we use should be in a closed loop of recycling.  

**Biodegradable polymers**

Biodegradable polymers are a special class of polymer that breaks down after its intended purpose by bacterial decomposition process to result in natural byproducts such as gases ($\ce{CO2}$, $\ce{N2}$), water, biomass, and inorganic salts. Some biodegradable polymers are produced from biomass, some are from fossil fuel. The polymers have chemical structures similar to naturally occurring polymers, thus can be degraded by microbes. Considering the price, available types which determines possible mechanical/physical properties, biodegradable polymers only contribute a small fraction of commercial products. More importantly, some products do need to be durable and thus cannot be produced using biodegradable polymers. 

**Train microbes to degrade synthetic polymers**

Example: Plastic-eating bacteria turn waste PET into useful starting materials for other products. *ACS Central Science* **2023**, DOI: 10.1021/acscentsci.3c00414. 

![](https://pubs.acs.org/cms/10.1021/acscentsci.3c00414/asset/images/large/oc3c00414_0008.jpeg =800x)

Limit: Perhaps polymers with chemical structures similar to naturally occuring polymers could be treated this way relatively easy. Otherwise, it could be very challenging. 

**Chemical recycling to monomers**

A process that breaks down polymers into monomers and then purifies them for repolymerization. In this way, it fulfills the goal of closed loop recycling. 

![](./img/RecycleToMonomer.png =600x)

*Chemistry A European Journal* **2023**, *29*, e202203635, DOI: 10.1002/chem.202203635.

The concept is nice, but very challenging. Many polymerization processes cannot go backward efficiently without significant side reactions. There are a few examples showing how to depolymerize some commonly used polymers in this manner or how to design new polymers following this principle to replace existing products. But in general, this field is new and in urgent need.
