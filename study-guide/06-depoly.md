## Destinies of polymer wastes{{attrs[#blk-9qxylcaovcsj]}}

The annual loss in value of single-use packaging waste — equating to 47% of polymer waste — is estimated at US$80–120 billion (corresponding to 95% of the embed- ded value), a figure that does not include externalized environmental costs. Recovered plastics are either valo- rized through primary (closed-loop) and secondary (cascade) recycling or, more commonly, combusted for energy production. However, most plastics are relegated to landfill or are leaked into the environment. If, at the outset of commodity polymer synthesis, we had known what we know now, how would we have designed our materials differently?(DOI: 10.1038/s41578-020-0190-4)

![Screenshot 2024-10-16 at 09.34.12](https://hackmd.io/_uploads/SykGyITyJg.png =600x)

Impediments to the recycling of commodity polymers include separation, impurities and degradation of the macromolecular structures, all of which can negatively affect the properties of recycled materials.

Chemical recycling to monomer (CRM). Material recycled in this way exhibits no loss in properties, creating an ideal, circular polymer economy. Frustratingly, the chemical success of these recycling efforts is roughly inversely proportional to the scale of their production. Although not commercially viable, CRM of nylon 6 proceeds with very high yields and selectivities; this is also true for other relatively low-volume resins, such as PTHF. Of the individually numbered resins, PS and PET are produced in the lowest volumes; although CRM of the former has selectivity issues, monomers can still be recovered in well above 90% yield, and PET is the most highly recycled resin. However, for the three polymers produced in largest volumes — PVC, PE and PP — CRM is either chemically impossible (PVC) or extremely challenging (polyolefins).

We will also discuss other meaningful depolymerization and recycling methods. 

Polyolefins have long constituted the largest segment of the polymer market, consistently amounting to more than half of global primary polymer production and waste. Consequently, the development of materials that serve as polyolefin replacements would have the largest potential economic and environmental impact.

To understand the thermodynamic and kinetic context of CRM, we need examine the energetics of polymerization and depolymerization.

## Thermodynamics{{attrs[#blk-a0cbir5bkp5q]}}

Polymerization reactions are usually exothermic. Why? The process of polymerizatin causes the loss of monomer translational freedom which reesults in a negative entropy change. To achieve a negative change in Gibbs free energy, the polymerization must be exergonic driven by enthalpy which must exceed the entropic penalty. 

$$\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S$$

Each polymerization system has a critical temperature ($T_c$) which is a function of $\Delta H /\Delta S$. At the critical temperature $\Delta G$ becomes zero and the polymerization is at equilibrium. Dilution of the reaction lowers $T_c$. In rare cases, such as for $\ce{S8}$ and $\ce{(Me2SiO)4}$, $\Delta S$ is positive and $\Delta H$ is very small; in these instances, $T_c$ is known as the floor temperature. We consider the former case only. Polymerization with high monomer conversion must be under $T_c$, while complete reversion to monomer requires a temperature above $T_c$. 

![Screenshot 2024-10-16 at 09.58.55](https://hackmd.io/_uploads/BkkC4LTk1x.png =600x)

If the polymerization is highly exothermic, the $T_c$ must be high. It would cost a lot of energy to depolymerize the polymer. Hard to be economic and may lead to side products. Some could be usefu and some may be applied to produce other chemicals instead of the monomer. 

![Screenshot 2024-10-16 at 10.31.39](https://hackmd.io/_uploads/SyJd38a11x.png =600x)


If the polymerization has a $\Delta G$ close to 0, the $T_c$ must be low and the polymerization has be to carried out at low temperature. Thermodynamically, the polymer is not stable. But if chain end cap is applied, the polymer could remain stable even at elavated temperatures. One example is poly($\gamma$-butyrolactone) (PGBL) which has a $T_c$ of $\pu{-9 ^\circ C}$. End capped PGBL only begins to notably depolymerize near $\pu{200 ^\circ C}$. However, common metal halides (such as $\ce{CaCl2}$ and $\ce{ZnCl2}$) can catalyze the depolymerization and halve the energy barrier raising concern regarding the structural integrity of PGBL.

## Chemical recycling to monomers (CRM){{attrs[#blk-erlhf2yu5ql2]}}

Requirements of CRM:
- Achieve a delicate balance between the polymerization and depolymerization energetics.
- The polymer needs to remain stable under processing temperatures.
- End capping can help to enhance the stability of the polymer even above $T_c$, but can also hinder the potential for recycling, even a chain end hydrogen can kinetically trap the polymer from depolymerization. 
- Catalysis is crucial to the success of CRM. 
  
Tailored catalysis offers the potential to precisely define the temperature and prod- ucts of a depolymerization reaction. Hammond’s postulate implies that the best catalyst at the high temperatures ($\gg T_c$) needed for depolymerization may differ from the optimal polymerization catalyst. At these higher temperatures, effective polymerization catalysts might favour a competing pathway66 or thermally decompose. Investigation on depolymerization catalysis is needed. 

### Polyethylene and polypropylene

- Ideal for packaging
    - Lightweight
    - Durable
    - Flexible but pretty strong
    - Optical properties
    - Low cost starting materials
    - Highly efficient synthesis
 
Polyolefins degrade during melt processing owing to radical reactions that raise the molecular weight for PE and lower it for PP, leading to an increase in dispersity for both. Unless advances in recycling, nascent PCW purification technologies or other research innovations alter this dynamic, polyolefin waste will continue to grow in pro- portion to polymer production. CRM is not energetically accessible for polyolefins. It is necessary to find replacements.

### Other poly(alkene)s

**Poly($\alpha$-methylstyrene) (PAMS)** The formation of PAMS ($T_c = \pu{61 ^\circ C}$) involves a propagating tertiary radical that is relatively easily regenerated for depolymerization. PAMS reverts almost exclusively to monomer. However, PAMS has poor thermomechanical properties and has not notable commercial usage. 

**Polystyrene (PS)** is a widely used material but the depolymerization produces styrene, toluene, $\alpha$-methystyrene, 1,3-diphenylpropane and styrene dimer or trimer. 

**Poly(methyl methacrylate) (PMMA)** may present the strongest case for CRM of a polymer not made through ROP. PMMA is widely used, but differ from polyolefins. PMMA is brittle and highly transparent, which finds its own applications rather than replacement of polyolefins. It has the lowest $T_c$ among widely used polymers ($\pu{220 ^\circ C}$ for bulk PMMA, compared with, for example, $\pu{400 ^\circ C}$ for PE). Under an inert atmosphere, commercial PMMA heated to above $\pu{400 ^\circ C}$ produces methyl methacrylate in high yield, with trace by-products. Although potentially viable for industrial scrap, this process has not yet been effectively applied to PCW plastics, which are geo- graphically dispersed, commingled with other plastics and contaminated with other substances.

CRM is difficult for many existing polymers. It will not be easy to displace current commodity polymers with any of the new materials examined herein. Meaningful CRM also requires collection and sorting of polymer wastes first. Certain level of cleaning may also be necessary. 

### ROP based CRM

Any ring with functionality that allows for ROP could, in principle, produce a polymer capable of CRM.

**Polycarbonate**

Polymers derived from geometrically constrained fused-ring systems (**1**, **2**, **5** and **6**) revert to either the epoxide and $\ce{CO2}$ by RCDD or to the *cis*-fused carbonate by *c*-RCD. The former is a manifestation of CRM, whereas the latter has, thus far, been a reactive dead end. The second general observation is that relatively unconstrained trans- [4.3.0] carbonates (**3** and **4**) can cycle between polymer synthesis by ROP and monomer recovery by *t*-RCD.

![Screenshot 2024-10-16 at 12.14.21](https://hackmd.io/_uploads/SkZtNdaykg.png =600x)

![Screenshot 2024-10-16 at 12.14.53](https://hackmd.io/_uploads/HJ9i4dTykx.png =600x)

All these polycarbonates have high $T_g$ values ($\pu{110-130 ^\circ C}$) and mostly no $T_m$. They are not replacements of polyolefins. Several of the monomers have relatiely low price. Overall, it is not clear whether the polymers have the potential for large-scale applications. 

**Polyesters**

Poly(lactic acid) (PLA) has good mechanical properties (tensile strength 59 MPa) and high $T_m = \pu{165 ^\circ C}$, but more brittle than polyolefin give $T_g = \pu{55 ^\circ C}$ and only 7% elongation at break. PLA are synthesized through step-growth oligomerization of lactic acid, depolymerized to lactide and then repolymerize into PLA. PLA can be composted, while such process eliminates the energetic investiments. 

CRM is not viable for PLA to produce lactide, owing to side reactions. It is susceptible to elimination to yield acrylic acid, as well as epimerization of the $\alpha$-position. Off-grade factory PLA can be hydrolysed to lactic acid and resubmitted to the step-growth process.

The polyesters have low $T_g$ values and the potential to access higher values of $T_m$ through co-monomer incorporation, manipulation of tacticity and stereocomplex formation, although the latter has complications. Depolymerization of a stereocomplex returns a racemic mixture. 

The thermal properties of polymer **12** were explored, revealing a relatively low $T_g$ ($\pu{27 ^\circ C}$) and a high $T_m$ (centred at $\pu{69 ^\circ C}$) present only in the first heating. 

For the polymer, **14**, low to moderate $T_g$ values were found ($T_g = \pu{32-37 ^\circ C}$ or $\pu{67 ^\circ C}$, depending on R), but there was no $T_m$. 

Polymers derived from 17 have been more extensively characterized. Some polymer properties are an excellent match to those of polyolefins, including $T_m$ ($\pu{130–200 ^\circ C}$) and tensile strength (26–55 MPa). However, other properties, such as $T_g$ (~ $\pu{33–90 ^\circ C}$) and elongation at break (6–13%), are outside the desirable range. 

The lactones examined here are mostly petroleum derived, with 4-carbomethoxyvalerolactone **8** and thioester **13** being the notable exceptions. 

Both $\delta$-VL and $\varepsilon$-VL are produced industrially and are, consequently, available inexpensively at scale. Of the new-to-the-world polymers discussed here, the most thoroughly explored CRM process at present uses the fused GBLs **15** and **17**. The synthesis of these monomers is still complex and costly, despite having a higher atom economy and lower E-factor (total mass of waste/mass of product) than lactide.

More about polyesters from lactones, see [Macromolecules 2024, 57, 1919−1940](https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.macromol.3c01912).

To make polyoxymethylene homopolymer, anhydrous formaldehyde must be generated. Poly(1,3-Dioxolane) (PDXL) is a good candidate to replace current packaging materials but faces several difficulties. 

Polyacetals can be synthesized by ring opening polymerization of cyclic acetal monomers. 1,3-Dioxolane (DXL) is commercially synthesized in a single step on a large scale from common industrial C1 and C2 feedstocks — formaldehyde and ethylene glycol, respectively — both of which also have bioderived routes. However, consistently obtaining high molecular weight polyacetals has remained challenging because cyclic acetal polymerization using Brønsted or Lewis acid catalysts affords poor molecular weight control. In the 2021 Science paper, Coates and coworkers developed a reversible deactivation polymerization method for DXL to be able to achieve **high molecular weight**. This polymerization method works for a broad variety of cyclic acetal monomers. Low molecular weight PDXL (37.9 kDa), which is comparable to the highest previously reported molecular weight, demonstrates poor mechanical properties, with a $\delta_B$ of only 13.5 ± 1.3 MPa at 5 ± 0.3% strain. At 82.3 kDa, PDXL tensile properties increase significantly to $\delta_B$ = 33.3 ± 1.2 MPa and $\varepsilon_B$ = 640 ± 45%. At 180 kDa, PDXL showed high tensile strength, with $\delta_B$ = 40.4 ± 1.2 MPa and $\varepsilon_B$ = 720 ± 20%, revealing the impressive toughness and ductility of PDXL at high molecular weights.

#### Biodegradation

[*ACS Catal.* **2019**, *9*, 4089–4105](https://doi.org/10.1021/acscatal.8b05171)

Not only for chemically recycling of the polymer to monomers, but also means the microplastics and nanoplastics of PET could be treated. 

A striking recent report describes the isolation of the PET-assimilating bacterium Ideonella sakaiensis 201-F6, which was isolated from a PET-recycling factory126. This bacterium expresses two key enzymes that decompose PET, which serves as its major carbon and energy source. (*Science* **2016**, *351*, 1196-1199. DOI: 10.1126/science.aad635) In a later report, high efficiency was achieved. Over 10 hours, a minimum of 90 percent PET depolymerization into monomers, with a productivity of 16.7 grams of terephthalate per litre per hour (200 grams per kilogram of PET suspension, with an enzyme concentration of 3 milligrams per gram of PET). (*Nature* **2020**, *580*, 216–219. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-020-2149-4) Another report in 2022 utilized machine learning to engineer the hydrolase using a structure-based, machine learning algorithm to engineer a robust and active PET hydrolase. "Our mutant and scaffold combination (FAST-PETase: functional, active, stable and tolerant PETase) contains five mutations compared to wild-type PETase (N233K/R224Q/S121E from prediction and D186H/R280A from scaffold) and shows superior PET-hydrolytic activity relative to both wild-type and engineered alternatives12 between 30 and 50 °C and a range of pH levels. We demonstrate that untreated, postconsumer-PET from 51 different thermoformed products can all be almost completely degraded by FAST-PETase in 1 week. FAST-PETase can also depolymerize untreated, amorphous portions of a commercial water bottle and an entire thermally pretreated water bottle at 50 ºC." (*Nature* **2022**, *604*, 662–667. https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-022-04599-z). And another *Nature Communications* **2024** 15, 1417 https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-024-45662-9.

### Polymerization and depolymerization by metathesis reactions 

https://pubs.acs.org/doi/10.1021/jacs.1c11197

The four- and five-membered rings trans-fused to cyclooctene reduce the ring strain energies of the monomer, and the enthalpy changes of polymerizations are found to be in the range of −2.1 to −3.3 kcal mol–1. Despite the narrow range of enthalpy changes, the ceiling temperatures at 1.0 M span from 330 to 680 °C, due to the low entropy changes, ranging from −2.7 to −5.0 cal mol–1 K–1. Importantly, geminal substituents on the trans-five-membered cyclic acetal fused cyclooctenes are found to reduce the ceiling temperature by ∼300 °C, although they are not directly attached to the cyclooctene. The remote gem-disubstituent effect demonstrated here can be leveraged to promote depolymerization of the corresponding polymers and to tune their thermomechanical properties. A new possibility?
