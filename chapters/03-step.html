Polyamides

A polyamide is a polymer with repeating units linked by amide bonds which is naturally the reaction product from carboxylic acid and amine. Protein is a type of polyamide. Nylon is a family of synthetic polyamide, usually linking aliphatic or semi-aromatic groups.

Production

Nylon 66 and related polyamides are condensation polymers forms from equal parts of diamine and dicarboxylic acids. The numbers in the name indicates the monomer units involved. For example, 66 means the diamine is hexamethylene diamine which has 6 carbon atoms and the dicarboxylic acid is adipic acid which also has 6 carbon atoms. In case of Nylon 610, the diamine has 6 carbon atoms while the dicarboxylic acid has 10 carbon atoms. Similarly, there are products named Nylon 46, 410, 510, 612, 1010, etc. There are also Nylon products with only one number, such as Nylon 6 which is produced using an amino acid that has 6 carbon atom molecule with a carboxylic acid group at one end and an amino group the other end. Nylon 6 can also be produced by ring-opening polymerization of lactam.

In case of Nylon 66, the stoichiometric balance between reactive groups is readily obtained by precipitating the 1:1 ammonium salt from ethanol. This compound is sometimes called a nylon salt. The salt/polymer equilibrium is more favorable to the production of polymer than in the case of polyesters, so this reaction is often carried out in a sealed tube or autoclave at about 200 °C until a fairly high extent of reaction is reached; then the temperature is raised and the water driven off to attain high molecular weight polymer.

Properties

The planar amide (−CO−NH−\ce{-CO-NH -}−CO−NH−) groups are very polar, so nylon forms multiple hydrogen bonds among adjacent strands.

Below TmT_mTm​, amorphous regions alternate with regions which are lamellar crystals. The amorphous regions contribute elasticity, and the crystalline regions contribute strength and rigidity. Because the nylon backbone is so regular and symmetrical, especially if all the amide bonds are in the trans configuration, nylons often have high crystallinity and make excellent fibers.

Nylons are hygroscopic and will absorb or desorb moisture as a function of the ambient humidity.

All nylons are susceptible to hydrolysis, especially by strong acids, a reaction essentially the reverse of their synthesis. The molecular weight of nylon products so attacked drops, and cracks form quickly at the affected zones.

Applications

Nylon 66 is frequently used when high mechanical strength, rigidity, good stability under heat and/or chemical resistance are required. Fiber is the most important form of application but other types such as film and bulk plastics are also available.

Polyesters

Polyester is a category of polymers that contain one or two ester linkages in every repeat unit of their main chain. The most commonly used polyester is polyethylene terephthalate (PET). Polyesters include naturally occurring chemicals, such as in plants and insects. Natural polyesters and a few synthetic ones are biodegradable, but most synthetic polyesters are not. Synthetic polyesters are used extensively in clothing.

Production

Since the methyl ester of a dicarboxylic acid is often more soluble and easier to purify than the diacid itself, the dimethyl terephthalate process was employed first and lasted for a long time. Now, purified terephthalic acid is more widely available, thus the teraphthalic acid process is also used.

In the dimethyl terephthalate (DMT) process, DMT and excess ethylene glycol (MEG) are transesterified in the melt at 150–200 °C with a basic catalyst. Methanol (CHX3OH\ce{CH3OH}CHX3​OH) is removed by distillation to drive the reaction forward. Excess MEG is distilled off at higher temperature with the aid of vacuum. The second transesterification step proceeds at 270–280 °C, with continuous distillation of MEG as well.

In the terephthalic acid process, MEG and PTA are esterified directly at moderate pressure (2.7–5.5 bar) and high temperature (220–260 °C). Water is eliminated in the reaction, and it is also continuously removed by distillation.

Properties

PET is widely used as drink bottles and fibers for clothes. It has fairly good mechanical properties and durability, but less strong compared to Nylon.

PET is subject to degradation during processing. If the moisture level is too high, hydrolysis will reduce the molecular weight by chain scission, resulting in brittleness.

Polyimides

Polyimides are representative of a class of high temperature thermoplastics, sometimes termed engineering plastics. Polyimide is prepared from the reaction of a dianhydride with a diamine. One example is KaptonTM, which is produced using pyromellitic dianhydride and 4,4′-diaminodiphenyl ether.

Production

Polyimide is prepared from the reaction of a dianhydride with a diamine. One example is KaptonTM, which is produced using pyromellitic dianhydride and 4,4′-diaminodiphenyl ether. A complication arises that is common with highly aromatic polymers, namely that the resulting polymers become insoluble at very low degrees of polymerization. Thus, the polymer is usually synthesized in two steps. The first step results in a high molar mass polyamic acid. Secondly, high temperature is used to facilitate removal of water and residual solvent. The final polyimides are in most cases infusible and insoluble. Different dianhydrides and diamines can be used to prepare a variety of polyimides.

Properties

  • Stable across a wide range of temperatures from −269 ∘C\pu{-269 ^\circ C}−269 ∘C to 400 ∘C\pu{400 ^\circ C}400 ∘C;
  • good chemical resistance;
  • excellent mechanical properties;
  • inherently resistant to flame combustion;
  • characteristic orange/yellow color.

Kapton insulation ages poorly: an FAA study shows degradation in hot, humid environments or in the presence of seawater. It was found to have very poor resistance to mechanical wear, mainly abrasion within cable harnesses due to aircraft movement. Many aircraft models have had to undergo extensive rewiring modifications—sometimes completely replacing all the Kapton-insulated wiring—because of short circuits caused by the faulty insulation.

Applications

Test unit of the James Webb Space Telescope sunshield, made of aluminized Kapton.

Aluminized Kapton thermal cover was used on the Ultra Heavy Cosmic Ray Experiment.

Production

Kinetics

Assumption of equal reactivity : The reactivity of a functional group is independent of the size of the molecule containing it.

  • Early work by Carothers, Flory, and others explored the influence of molecular size on reactivity by studying esterification and saponification rates.
  • These reactions exhibit an initial dependence on molecular size for small molecules.
  • The rate constants rapidly converge to a constant value, independent of molecular size, as the molecules get larger.
  • In carboxylic acid esterification, for example, the rate constants vary for acetic, propionic, and butyric acids.
  • However, the rate constants become consistent for carboxylic acids with 4 to 18 carbon atoms.
  • This principle, initially observed in non-polymeric systems, has been extended to polymerization reactions.

Polyesterification

The kinetics and rates of step-growth polymerization can be described using a polyesterification mechanism. The simple esterification is an acid-catalyzed process in which protonation of the acid is followed by interaction with the alcohol to produce an ester and water. A few assumptions needed with this kinetic model:

  1. Water (or any other condensation product) is efficiently removed;
  2. The functional group reactivities are independent of chain length;
  3. Each step only involves one alcohol and one acid.

Sol-gel fractions

In polymer science, the terms “sol” and “gel” fractions refer to two distinct components of a polymer network, typically encountered when studying crosslinked polymers or during processes like gelation.

The sol fraction is the portion of a polymer sample that remains soluble in a solvent. It consists of low molecular weight chains, uncrosslinked molecules, or loosely connected polymer fragments that don’t form part of the larger, interconnected network. Essentially, these are the bits that can still dissolve and be extracted because they aren’t tied into the permanent structure. Think of it as the “loose” or “free-floating” part of the system.

The gel fraction, on the other hand, is the insoluble portion—the crosslinked, three-dimensional network of polymer chains that forms a solid-like structure. Once the polymer reaches a certain level of crosslinking (past the gel point), it transitions into this gel state, where it can no longer dissolve but might swell in a solvent. This is the “fixed” or “solid” part that gives the material its structural integrity.

These concepts are key in understanding how polymers behave, especially in applications like hydrogels, rubbers, or coatings. For example, in a crosslinked rubber, the gel fraction determines its strength and elasticity, while the sol fraction might indicate incomplete crosslinking or leftover monomers. Researchers often measure these fractions experimentally by soaking a sample in a solvent and weighing what dissolves (sol) versus what remains (gel).
